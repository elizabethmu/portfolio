School Catering


Multi-tenant web app for senior citizens

Summary

Most of us view technology as necessity. For senior citizens that are new to computers, however, technology is a simple tool that gets their job done.

This is my story of redesigning School Catering — a multi-tenant web app that empowers people above the age of 60 to process catering orders like a breeze.

For confidentiality purposes, I have omitted and obfuscated confidential information in this case study. These designs are a reinterpretation of the original.

Platform

Web, SaaS, B2B, B2C

Duration

3.5 weeks

When

2018

Business Problem

Revenue dropped by 30% because some clients have left. Support staff were working 50 hours a week answering phone calls and emails. We hypothesized that our product was difficult to use.

Goal

Increase customer satisfaction. Increase revenue.

My Role

I was the sole product designer on an agile team of 2 developers and 2 customer support specialists. I was brought in to the team in 2018 to improve the overall design. My responsibilities included research, UX, UI, and print design. I collaborated with customer support on features and engineers on system compatibility.

Design Principles

Below are the design principles I followed for this project. Some are from Nielsen Norman’s heuristics and some are my own established specifically for School Catering:


Consistency Use the same icons and words so that our users don't get confused.
Readability Because 80% of our clients are senior citizens who are experiencing weakening vision, I made font sizes much bigger than what you would normally see on a web app.
System Feedback Provide reassurance when users complete an action. For example, when users place an order for 20 sandwiches, the application will display 20 as a confirmation.
Desktop Only I specifically designed School Catering to be not mobile responsive. It's against the norm but for a good reason.
  1. Our customers only use computers for work.
  2. Our system had a lot of spaghetti legacy code that requied time to fix.
  3. We had limited time to push the new design.

User Relations Chart

My company sells School Catering to school districts all over the country. Within each school district, our product served three groups of users.

I wanted to understand not only the relationship between our users and our product, but also the relationships among our users. Based on my research, lunch ladies used the application most frequently while chefs had a unique need for print design. School deans were the decision makers when it came to purchasing the product, so we had to meet their needs first to make a sale.

Low-Fi Prototype

After I had a good idea of the user interactions, I turned pencil sketches into quick prototypes. My debate was whether to use modal or a flat design.

Modal: Select items on a modal

Flat: Display options below a food item

Discussion with Developers

I presented my low-fi prototypes to engineers to discuss technical limitations and to find out their preferences. All engineers voted for modal.

Modal Won

All engineers voted for modal. Everyone else at the office also voted for modal. We liked modal because it looked clean. When I presented both prototypes to existing customers, they seemed fairly neutral towards both designs.

But We Implemented Flat

Unfortunately, we ended up implementing a flat design because adding a modal meant changing a lot of the legacy code in our system. This was not realistic under our deadline. Luckily, when I presented both prototypes to our customers, there was not a strong preference. After the feature release, our customers told us that they appreciated us keeping the flat design, because that was the “look” of the software they were familiar with.

User Reaction

After the feature release, our customers told us that although they thought a modal looked “cool”, they appreciated us keeping the flat design because the previous product had a flat design and that was something they were familiar with. Had we implemented a modal, our users would have to go through a learning curve.

Visual Design

Duration

1 day

Considering that 80% of my users are 60+ years old and experiencing aging vision, I wanted to ensure Catering caters to this demographic. Based on my research, their biggest need was to make everything easy to see. This means big fonts, big icons, and a color palette that resonates with fresh food.

Colors

I drew inspiration from the existing logo, which looked like an green apple. My goal was to sprinkle our brand green in the application to strengthen brand recognition and create familiarity. Because all school districts had to meet accessibility requirements, I made sure all of our colors were AAA compliant.

Iconography

I used icons from the materials library for three reasons:

  1. It is a comprehensive library.
  2. Our users easily understood what each icon meant.
  3. Our engineers could easily plug the icons into their code.
  4. This was the most time efficient method under a deadline.

Typography

The first improvement I did was to increase size to at least 18px. Prevoius fonts were smaller than 11px and our users often had to zoom in the page to 150% to be able to see the content. I used Lato for two reasons:

  1. It is a standard sans-serif font that is easy to read for our user demographic.
  2. I didn’t have much time to experiment with font pairing.

Retrospective

The biggest challenge my team faced was the tension between velocity, being thoughtful, and system compatiability. The business owner had to play catch up with the competitive landscape, engineering was fighting legacy code, and I had to finalize and deliver product elements days before engineering built them.

Thanks to an aggressive release date, conceptualizing a “complete experience” had to wait. In the meanwhile, I also got pulled into other design projects and had to revisit Catering when I was on a completely different train of thought. So I put all my focus on laying a solid foundation and making the MVP easy to learn and easy to build.

Build design literacy and share a common goal

Nobody at the company knew product design. Since my team had never worked together and I was the first and only product designer, cohesion and trust were incredibly important to establish early.

In the beginning, I often got asked to show a visual representation of what the new product would look like. This means jumping right into a high fidelity prototype without going through any of the steps to get there.

I started doing design literacy and showed my team how a good design process can lead to a strong product. This proved incredibly helpful as my colleagues picked up concepts such as user flows and why they were important. It also gave me breathing room to do the necessary prep work like researching our customers, mapping out the flows and testing the prototypes. It helped my team understand what my role was, what our goal was, and saved myself from pushing pixels just to make something pretty.

User research is never too little

It’s easy to keep my head down and push wireframes when a deadline is around the corner, but what good is it to deliver a bad product quickly?

As the sole product designer and without a budget for research, it was my responsibility to understand our customers quickly and accurately. Gathering notes from the support team, profiling user segments, and testing prototypes with internal folks was as lean as it gets. To make my research as effective as possible, I initiated a few conference calls with existing customers and had the chance to present my designs and hear their thoughts (with permission from the company, of course). It was extremely helpful. All of our feature releases have received overwhelmingly positive feedback.

Step up and say no when necessary

One of the conflicts of interests was between customers and developers. Because different school districts need to fulfill different requirements, I divided input fields to mandatory and optional. Mandatory means it's the basic requirement for all schools. Optional means it's an additional requirement your school dean has added just for your school. A few of our biggest customers (in terms of revenue) wanted to make all of their requirements mandatory across all school districts. This proposal could affect many other school districts. I stepped in as a product manager and explained the consequences. Our customers gracefully accepted me saying no and kept the business with us. Our developers appreciated me speaking up for them to decline unnecessary feature requests.

Impact

Customers called in to compliment our new design and revenue went up. I did think that if we had a bit more time, we would have arrived at a better redesign.
